Words Every Beginner Pronounces Wrong in German

German is generally more phonetic and predictable than English—but some letter combinations trip up Portuguese speakers, because their natural intuition leads them to the wrong pronunciation. Here are the most common pitfalls.

"W" isn’t "u," it’s "v"

Wasser (water) isn’t pronounced "uasser"—the German "w" sounds like the "v" in Portuguese. Wein (vinho) sounds like “vain,” not “uain.” This is probably the number one source of confusion for beginners.

"V" sounds like "f" most of the time

This is counterintuitive for those who are used to "w" sounding like "v": the German "v" usually sounds like "f." Vater (father) is pronounced "fáter," not "váter." The exception is words of foreign origin, such as Vase (vase), which retain the “v” sound.

Mein Vater trinkt gern Wein.
My dad likes to drink wine.

"Z" sounds like "ts"

Zeit (time) is not pronounced "zait"—it sounds like "tsait." Whenever a "z" appears in German, it carries this "ts" sound attached to it; it’s never the hissing "z" we use in Portuguese.

"S" before a vowel sounds like "z"

Sonne (sol) sounds like "zone," not "sone." When "s" comes before a vowel at the beginning of a syllable, it becomes a "z" sound—only when doubled ("ss") or in other positions does it retain the normal "s" sound.

Sch and “st/sp” at the beginning of a word

Sch sound like the English “sh” — Schule (school) sounds like “shule.” And when “st” or “sp” appear at the beginning of a word, the “s” also becomes a “sh” sound: Stadt (city) sounds like “shtadt,” Sprache (language) sounds like “shprache.”

Why it’s good to learn this early

Unlike English, these rules are consistent—once you learn them, they always work, with no random exceptions. It’s a matter of reprogramming your reading instinct just once, not memorizing word by word forever.
