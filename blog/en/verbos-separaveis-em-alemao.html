Separable verbs in German: why "aufstehen" becomes "steht...auf"

You look up the verb “aufstehen” (to get up) in the dictionary, memorize what it means, and the first time you see it in a real sentence, the auf has simply disappeared from the beginning and reappeared on its own at the end: Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf (I get up at seven o’clock). To anyone who doesn’t know this is normal, it looks like a typo.

What exactly is a separable verb

Many German verbs are formed by a prefix + a base verb: auf + “stehen” = “aufstehen.” In main clauses, this prefix separates from the verb and moves to the end of the sentence, while the conjugated verb usually remains in the second position. It’s as if the verb were “taken apart” every time you use it in a simple sentence.

Ich rufe meine Mutter heute Abend an .
I’m calling my mom tonight. (anrufen = to call)

How do you know if a verb is separable

A practical tip: if the prefix is stressed (the stressed syllable falls on it when you say the infinitive—AUF-stehen, AN-rufen, MIT-kommen), the verb is separable. If the prefix isn’t stressed (such as be-, ge-, ver-, ent- — verSTEHEN, beKOMMEN), the verb is inseparable and never separates from the verb.

And when it does NOT separate

In subordinate clauses (those beginning with weil, dass, wenn) and in infinitives with zu, the separable verb becomes whole again, joined together: weil ich um sieben Uhr aufstehe (because I get up at seven o’clock)—note that “aufstehe” becomes a single word again. This confuses a lot of people who learned the rule only for simple sentences.

Why this is easier to hear than to explain

The written rule seems complicated, but anyone who hears many sentences with separable verbs picks up the pattern quickly, almost without realizing it—the brain learns to “expect” the prefix at the end, just as it learned to expect the verb at the end of subordinate clauses.
