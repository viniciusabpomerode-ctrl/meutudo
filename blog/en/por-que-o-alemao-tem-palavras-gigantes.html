Why German Has Giant Words (and How This Helps, Not Hinders)

German words with 40, 50, or 60 letters periodically go viral on the internet as proof that the language is “impossible”—but there’s a very simple (and useful) logic behind this phenomenon.

The rule: put everything together, without spaces

Unlike Portuguese, which uses prepositions to connect nouns (“law on beef labeling”), German simply combines nouns into a single word: Rindfleischetikettierungsüberwachungsaufgabenübertragungsgesetz (a real German law on the delegation of tasks related to the supervision of beef labeling) is just that—word for word, strung together.

Das ist ein langes Wort, aber es macht Sinn.
This is a long word, but it makes sense.

Why this really helps learners

Once you know the smaller parts, the “meaning” of the giant word is a matter of logical deduction, not new memorization. This is radically different from languages where long, complex words (such as technical terms in Portuguese derived from Latin or Greek) have no obvious connection to everyday words.

True giant words are rare in everyday life

Viral words with 60+ letters are almost always extremely specific legal or administrative terms—everyday German uses much shorter and more practical compounds (as we’ve already seen in Handschuh, Kühlschrank, etc.), rarely exceeding 3–4 connected parts.

A real (and curious) record

The word Rindfleischetikettierungsüberwachungsaufgabenübertragungsgesetz was officially taken out of use when the law it referred to was repealed in 2013—proof that even Germans themselves recognize the practical limits of these giant constructions.

How does this change your relationship with new vocabulary?

Instead of fearing long words, start mentally “breaking down” any unfamiliar compound word into its parts—it’s a skill that improves quickly with practice and turns “intimidating” vocabulary into a logical puzzle.
