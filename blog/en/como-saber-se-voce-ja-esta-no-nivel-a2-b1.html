How to know if you’re already at level A2, B1... in German

“I’ve been studying for 6 months, but I don’t know if I’m already at A2 or B1” is a common question—mainly because many people learn vocabulary and grammar without ever comparing it to a real level reference. The Common European Framework of Reference (CEFR) exists precisely to resolve this uncertainty.

What each level actually means in practice

  • A1: understands and uses basic everyday expressions (introducing oneself, asking for simple information).
  • A2: can use phrases and expressions related to areas of immediate priority (shopping, work, family) in simple, direct conversations.
  • B1: handles most travel situations, describes experiences, and expresses opinions in a simple way.
  • B2: converses with reasonable fluency with native speakers; understands complex texts on concrete and abstract topics.
  • C1: expresses themselves fluently and spontaneously; uses the language flexibly for social, academic, and professional purposes.
  • C2: understands practically everything they read or hear with near-native accuracy.

The practical test that usually reveals your level

A quick and honest way to find out where you stand: watch a video or listen to an audio clip in German without subtitles. If you understand the general idea but miss some details, you’re probably between A2 and B1. If you can follow almost everything but get stuck on more specific vocabulary or abstract topics, you’re already at B1/B2.

Why this matters when choosing what to study

Knowing your actual level helps you avoid two common pitfalls: reviewing content that’s too basic (wasting time) or jumping into content that’s too advanced too soon (leading to frustration). It’s the difference between studying efficiently and studying in the dark.
