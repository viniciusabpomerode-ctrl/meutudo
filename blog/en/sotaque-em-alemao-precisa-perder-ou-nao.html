Accent in German: Should You Lose It or Not?

Many people who study German develop a specific anxiety about their accent—as if speaking with a Brazilian accent were a sign of failure in learning the language. It’s worth separating this into two very different things: accent and clarity.

An accent isn’t the same as a mistake

Having an accent means that your intonation, rhythm, and some sounds are influenced by your native language—this is normal and expected for almost anyone who learns a language after childhood. It doesn’t mean you’re “speaking incorrectly”; it means you have a background, and that comes through in your speech, just as it does with any foreigner speaking Portuguese.

Ein Akzent ist kein Fehler.
An accent isn’t a mistake.

What really matters: being understood

The practical goal of learning to pronounce words correctly isn’t to sound 100% native—it’s to be easily understood by any German speaker, without requiring extra effort on the listener’s part. This is much more achievable (and much faster to achieve) than eliminating every trace of an accent.

When an accent actually gets in the way

The real problem isn’t “having an accent”—it’s when specific pronunciation errors change the meaning of a word or make it too difficult to understand. Confusing “ü” with “u,” for example, can turn müssen (to have to/to need) into something unrecognizable. These specific cases are worth practicing; the general accent, however, is not.

Many Germans find a foreign accent charming

It’s worth clearing up a misconception: most Germans are welcoming and even find it endearing when a foreigner speaks German with an accent, as long as they can communicate clearly. There’s no shame in it—it’s a natural part of learning a language as an adult.

Where should you focus your energy, then?

Prioritize clarity in the sounds that change meaning (ü, ö, ch, the guttural “r” in the right context) and don’t waste energy trying to eliminate all the musicality of your native language from your speech—that comes naturally over time, if it comes at all.
