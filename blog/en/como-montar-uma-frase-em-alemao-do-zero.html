How to Construct a Sentence in German from Scratch

After learning vocabulary and verb conjugations, many people still get stuck when trying to construct their own sentences from scratch—because they try to translate word for word from Portuguese, and the word order just doesn’t match. A simple mental guide solves this.

The golden rule: the verb always comes second

In main (affirmative) clauses, the conjugated verb always occupies the second position in the sentence—no matter what comes before it. Ich gehe heute ins Kino (I’m going to the movies today) and Heute gehe ich ins Kino (Today I’m going to the movies) both have the verb in the second position, even when the order of the other words is reversed.

Morgen fahre ich nach Berlin.
Tomorrow I’m traveling to Berlin.

The Step-by-Step Guide

  • Choose what you want to emphasize (time, subject, place) and put it in the first position.
  • Place the conjugated verb right after that (position 2, always).
  • Place the subject, if it wasn’t the first element.
  • Add complements: usually time, then mode, then place (TMP — Temporal, Modal, Lokal).
  • If there’s a second verb (infinitive or participle), it goes at the end of the sentence.

Why does this seem too rigid at first?

The flexibility of placing different elements in the first position is actually an advantage of German—it allows you to shift emphasis without changing the basic meaning, simply by rearranging what comes before the verb. What requires adaptation is breaking the habit of constructing sentences in the “natural” order of Portuguese and relying on the rule of the verb in the second position.

How to practice this without memorizing the rule

Instead of trying to consciously apply the rule to every sentence, listening to and repeating complete sentences several times builds intuition—after enough time, the correct order “sounds right” even before you think about the rule.
