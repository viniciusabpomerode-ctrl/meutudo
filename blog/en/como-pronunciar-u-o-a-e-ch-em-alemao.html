How to Pronounce Ü, Ö, Ä, and "ch" in German

There are four sounds in German that simply don’t exist in Portuguese, and they’re exactly the ones that make any beginner hesitate before trying to speak out loud: the umlauts (ü, ö, ä) and the famous “ch.” The good news is that none of them are as difficult as they seem—you just need a visual explanation, not an auditory one.

Ü — the “u” with an “i” mouth shape

To make the “ü” sound (as in über or müde), round your lips as if you were going to say “u,” but position your tongue as if you were going to say “i.” The result is a sound somewhere between the two—very similar to the “u” in the French word “tu.” Practicing by saying i... u... ü very slowly, without moving your tongue, helps you feel the difference.

Ö — the “o” with an “e” mouth shape

Same logic as for “ü”: round your lips as for “o,” but keep your tongue in the “e” position. Words like schön (beautiful) and können (to be able to/to manage) use this sound constantly—it’s worth practicing these two specific words until they sound natural.

Ich möchte gern über die schöne Küche sprechen.
I’d like to talk about the beautiful kitchen.

Ä — the easiest of the three

“Ä” is the simplest: it sounds almost exactly like the open “é” in Portuguese, as in “café.” Words like Mädchen (girl) and Väter (parents) use this sound—if you already pronounce the open “é” naturally, you already know how to pronounce the ä.

The “ch”: two different sounds disguised as one

Here’s the trick that nobody explains properly: the German “ch” has two different sounds, depending on the vowel before it. After a, o, u—as in Bach or Buch—the sound is guttural, made deep in the throat (similar to the Spanish “j”). After e, i, ä, ö, ü, or a consonant—as in ich or Milch—the sound is softer, almost a faint “sh” made near the teeth.

Why listening repeatedly is more effective than just reading the rules

Reading the explanation helps you understand the logic, but what really makes the sound “stick” is listening to the same word several times in a row and trying to imitate it—your mouth needs to develop the muscle memory, not just your brain to understand the rule.
