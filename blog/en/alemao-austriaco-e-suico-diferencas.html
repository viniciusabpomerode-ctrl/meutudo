German, Austrian, and Swiss: Same Language, Very Different Accents

Germany, Austria, and the German-speaking part of Switzerland share the same official language—but anyone studying Standard German (Hochdeutsch) may be surprised to discover that understanding a Swiss person speaking naturally is a challenge in itself, even after years of study.

Austrian German: Melodic Accent and Unique Vocabulary

The German spoken in Austria (Österreichisches Deutsch) has a more melodic intonation, some different vowel sounds, and a fair amount of unique vocabulary—especially when it comes to food. Batata is Erdapfel in Austria, not Kartoffel as in Germany. Janeiro is Jänner, not Januar.

Grüß Gott! (Áustria/Bavária) — Guten Tag! (Alemanha padrão)
Both mean Bom dia/Boa tarde—the first is regional

Swiss German: almost a language of its own in speech

The Schweizerdeutsch (Swiss German) spoken in everyday life is so different from Standard German that many Germans from Germany have real difficulty understanding a conversation between Swiss people—to the point that Swiss TV often subtitles interview programs in Standard German so the rest of the German-speaking world can understand them.

But formal writing is practically the same

The good news: in formal written contexts (newspapers, documents, work emails), the three countries use a very similar form of Standard German, with minor spelling differences (Switzerland, for example, does not use “ß,” writing “ss” instead). Anyone learning Standard German can read a Swiss or Austrian newspaper without much difficulty.

Why this shouldn’t worry you

If you’re learning Standard German (the variety taught in most courses), you’ll be able to communicate perfectly well in all three countries in any formal situation or with people from outside the region—the challenge of understanding strong regional accents is something even Germans from different regions face when talking to one another.
