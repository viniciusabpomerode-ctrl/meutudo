German idiomatic expressions: literal translation vs. actual meaning

Every language has expressions that make no sense at all when translated word for word—and German has some particularly creative ones. Understanding the actual meaning (not the literal translation) is essential to avoid getting completely lost in a conversation.

Ich verstehe nur Bahnhof

Literal translation: “I only understand train station.” Actual meaning: “I don’t understand a thing”—used when someone is completely lost in a conversation or explanation, without understanding a single word of what’s being said.

Tut mir leid, ich verstehe nur Bahnhof.
Sorry, but I didn’t understand a thing.

Da liegt der Hund begraben

Literal translation: “That’s where the dog is buried.” Actual meaning: “That’s where the real problem lies” / “That’s the main issue”—used to point out the root cause of a problem, similar to “that’s where the problem lies” in Portuguese.

Ich glaub, mein Schwein pfeift

Literal translation: “I think my pig is whistling.” Actual meaning: an expression of extreme surprise, like “I can’t believe what I’m seeing/hearing”—used when something is so unexpected that it seems surreal.

Tomaten auf den Augen haben

Literal translation: “to have tomatoes in one’s eyes.” Actual meaning: “to fail to see something obvious”—equivalent to “not being able to see a hand in front of one’s face.”

Alles hat ein Ende, nur die Wurst hat zwei

Literal translation: “Everything has an end, only the sausage has two.” Actual meaning: a humorous way of saying that everything eventually ends—used lightly, sort of like a funny philosophical consolation.

Why learning idioms is different from learning vocabulary

No word-for-word dictionary prepares you for these expressions—they only make sense when learned as a complete unit, with context. It’s exactly the kind of content that listening in real-life context (not reading a list) teaches best.
