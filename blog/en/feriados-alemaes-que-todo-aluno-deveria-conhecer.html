German Holidays Every German Student Should Know

Unlike Brazil, where national holidays are practically the same throughout the country, Germany has holidays that vary from state to state—a direct reflection of the country’s highly regionalized religious and political history.

Tag der Deutschen Einheit (October 3)

German Unity Day celebrates the reunification of West and East Germany in 1990, following the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989. It is the only national holiday celebrated equally across all 16 German states—and carries enormous historical and emotional significance for the country.

Am 3. Oktober feiern wir die Deutsche Einheit.
On October 3, we celebrate German Unity.

Religious holidays that vary by state

States with a Catholic majority (such as Bavaria and North Rhine-Westphalia) have holidays like Fronleichnam (Corpus Christi) and Allerheiligen (All Saints’ Day), which states with a Protestant majority (such as Berlin or Hamburg) do not have. This comes as a surprise to many foreigners who expect that “a holiday is a holiday throughout the entire country.”

Reformationstag (October 31)

A Protestant holiday celebrating the day Martin Luther is said to have posted his 95 theses on the door of the church in Wittenberg in 1517—the starting point of the Protestant Reformation. Celebrated mainly in the northern and eastern states of the country.

Karfreitag: the “silent” holiday

Good Friday (Karfreitag) holds a special status in Germany: in many states, it is prohibited by law to hold dance events, play loud music, or provide public entertainment on this day, as a sign of religious respect—something that surprises many foreigners accustomed to holidays without such restrictions.

Why it’s worth learning this vocabulary

Knowing the names of holidays helps you understand why stores close on unexpected dates, why work conversations mention “long weekend” (Brückentag), and makes it easier to plan trips and your daily routine without any surprises.
