How School Works in Germany

One of the most common culture shocks for those moving to Germany with children (or who are simply interested in the system) is discovering that German children are sorted into different educational tracks much earlier than in Brazil—usually at age 10.

The separation that surprises foreigners

After Grundschule (elementary school, through 4th grade in most states), students are directed toward one of three (or more) different paths: Hauptschule (shorter, focused on rapid vocational training), Realschule (intermediate track), and Gymnasium (longer track, focused on preparing for college). This decision, made so early, is criticized by many educators for reinforcing social inequality—but it remains the standard model in most of the country.

Mein Sohn geht aufs Gymnasium.
My son is enrolled in Gymnasium (a track that prepares students for college).

The Abitur: the college entrance exam that isn’t quite a college entrance exam

Those who pass the Gymnasium go on to take the Abitur, a final exam that serves as both a certificate of completion and the primary criterion for admission to universities—unlike the Brazilian college entrance exam, there is no separate exam for each university; the Abitur (along with the cumulative grade from the last few years of school) is sufficient to qualify the student.

Ausbildung: the path for which Brazil has no direct equivalent

One of the German system’s greatest distinguishing features is the Ausbildung—dual vocational training, which combines theoretical classes with paid work at a partner company, typically lasting 2 to 3 years. It is a socially respected path (not viewed as a “second choice”), very common for technical professions, commerce, and industry.

Why does this matter for those studying German?

Understanding this vocabulary (Grundschule, Gymnasium, Abitur, Ausbildung) is essential for any conversation about education, careers, or life plans in Germany—these are words that come up all the time in everyday conversations about children, work, and the future.
