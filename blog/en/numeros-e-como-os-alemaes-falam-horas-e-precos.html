Numbers and How Germans Say Times and Prices

Numbers seem like the simplest vocabulary to learn—until you discover that German organizes compound numbers differently from Portuguese and expresses times in a way that confuses many people at first.

Compound numbers: the unit comes before the tens

In Portuguese, “vinte e um” follows the obvious order (twenty, then one). In German, it’s the opposite: “einundzwanzig” literally means “one-and-twenty” (ein + und + zwanzig). This applies to any compound number: 47 is “siebenundvierzig” (seven-and-forty).

Es ist Viertel vor drei.
It’s a quarter to three (literally: a quarter before three).

How Germans tell the time

In addition to the direct system (15:45 = fünfzehn Uhr fünfundvierzig), everyday spoken German frequently uses the “quarters and half” system relative to the next hour, not the current hour: “halb drei” doesn’t mean “two-and-a-half”—it means “halfway to three,” that is, 14:30. This is one of the points that confuses beginners the most, because it seems counterintuitive coming from Portuguese.

Prices: Comma and Period Swapped

Like much of Europe, German uses a comma to separate decimals and a period to separate thousands—the opposite of the American standard (but the same as Brazilian Portuguese, so this part doesn’t change much for those who are already used to it). 1.234,56 € means one thousand two hundred thirty-four euros and fifty-six cents.

Why practicing numbers out loud matters

Numbers are one of the few types of vocabulary that you really NEED to process in real time and without hesitation—in a store, on the phone, or when giving directions. It’s worth specifically training this fluency, separate from the rest of your vocabulary.

A practical tip

Practice saying the time out loud using real clocks around you throughout the day—it’s a simple exercise that consistently builds fluency with numbers without requiring any special materials.
