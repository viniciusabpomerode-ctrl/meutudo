How Many Words Do You Need to Get By in German

The idea that “I need to know thousands of words before I can have a conversation” discourages many people right from the start—but the actual figures from applied linguistics show something much more encouraging.

The numbers that really matter

  • ~300 words: cover most basic survival situations (travel, simple shopping, self-introduction).
  • ~1,000 words: typically account for about 75–80% of any ordinary everyday conversation, according to lexical frequency studies.
  • ~2,000–3,000 words: a level considered comfortable for conversation (approximately B1/B2), covering the overwhelming majority of everyday situations.
  • ~10,000+ words: advanced/near-native fluency level (C1/C2), including technical vocabulary and nuances.
Man braucht nicht alle Wörter, um sich zu verständigen.
You don’t need to know every single word to communicate.

Why does Pareto’s Law apply here?

A small group of extremely common words (articles, auxiliary verbs, prepositions, pronouns) appears in the overwhelming majority of spoken sentences—mastering these first yields much greater practical benefits than memorizing rare vocabulary too early.

Word quality matters more than quantity

Knowing 500 high-frequency words (used all the time) handles far more real-life situations than knowing 2,000 random low-frequency words (rare, technical, or overly specific vocabulary too early on).

How this changes your study practice

Prioritize learning the words that appear most frequently in the language—common verbs, connectors, vocabulary for everyday situations—before worrying about niche or rare vocabulary.
