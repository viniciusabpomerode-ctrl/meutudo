Why the German verb goes to the end of the sentence

You’re comfortably reading a simple German sentence, and suddenly one appears with weil (because) or dass (that) in the middle—and the verb, which you expected right after the subject, has disappeared. It ended up at the very end of the sentence, sometimes after a dozen words. This is one of the most common surprises for Portuguese speakers learning German.

The rule behind the confusion

In main clauses, German behaves similarly to Portuguese: subject, verb, object. But in subordinate clauses—those that begin with conjunctions like weil, dass, wenn, obwohl—the conjugated verb is pushed to the end. It’s a fixed rule, with no exceptions, called Verbendstellung (verb final position).

Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich krank bin .
I’m staying home because I’m sick.

Why does this rule exist?

From a historical perspective, German preserves an older structure found in Germanic languages, in which the subordinate clause was treated almost like an isolated “block” within the main clause—and the verb, by convention, closes that block. There’s no logical reason you need to understand in order to use it correctly; it’s a matter of structure, not semantics.

The real problem: understanding long sentences by ear

The difficulty isn’t so much writing—you can pause, think, and organize the sentence. The problem is listening to someone speaking quickly and having to keep the subject and object in your head, waiting for the verb to appear only at the end to understand the full meaning of the sentence. It’s as much short-term memory training as it is grammar training.

How to really practice this

The only way that actually works is to listen to lots of subordinate clauses being spoken at a normal pace until your brain stops “waiting” for the verb in the wrong place. Reading alone isn’t enough—you need to train your auditory expectations.
