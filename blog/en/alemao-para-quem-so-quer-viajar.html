German for those who just want to travel: what you really need to know

If your goal is just to enjoy a relaxing trip through Germany, Austria, or Switzerland, you don’t need to be fluent—you need a small, well-chosen set of phrases that cover 90% of real-life travel situations.

The Real Essentials (No Nonsense)

  • Hallo / Guten Tag — hi / good morning (universal greeting)
  • Danke / Bitte — thank you / please
  • Entschuldigung — excuse me / sorry
  • Sprechen Sie Englisch? — do you speak English? (essential for more complex situations)
  • Wie viel kostet das? — how much does that cost?
Einmal zum Bahnhof, bitte.
A ticket to the station, please.

Why it’s still worth the effort

Even though almost everyone in tourist cities speaks English, trying basic phrases in German first usually results in a warmer welcome—it’s seen as a sign of respect for the local culture, not just a practical necessity.

Translation apps: when to use them, when not to

A translation app on your phone can handle emergencies and complex situations—but relying on it for everything takes away from the experience of real interaction. A good strategy is to use memorized phrases for simple, everyday interactions (ordering food, saying hello) and reserve the translator for more specific situations.

Is it worth investing a little more time?

If you travel frequently to German-speaking regions, investing 2–3 weeks in studying the basics (even if you don’t aim for fluency) completely changes the quality of your experience—from “completely dependent tourist” to “visitor who can handle most situations on their own.”
