German R vs. Portuguese R: How to Pronounce It Correctly

The "r" is probably the sound that causes the most uncertainty for those starting to learn German—because none of the variations of the "r" in Portuguese (neither the Rio variant nor the one from the interior of São Paulo) exactly matches the standard German sound.

There are, in fact, two “correct” versions

Standard German (Hochdeutsch) uses a guttural “r,” produced deep in the throat, similar to the sound you make when gargling—technically called a “uvular r.” This is the sound that most courses teach as “the correct one.” But there is also a softer, almost vowel-like variant used at the end of a syllable (as in Vater or “besser”), which sounds almost like a weak “a” at the end of the word.

Rot, Reise, Regen — Vater, besser, wieder
Red, trip, rain — dad, better, again

How to practice the guttural “r”

One technique that helps a lot: practice gargling with water (literally) to feel where the sound is produced in your throat, then try to reproduce the same point of articulation without water, using only air. Another option is to start by trying to make the sound of the French “r” in Paris—it’s very similar to the German one.

Why it doesn’t have to be perfect

Germans from different regions pronounce the “r” in different ways—in southern Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, it’s common to hear an “r” that’s more like a rolled “r,” similar to the Spanish or Italian one. There isn’t ONE “correct” way, even among native speakers themselves—your goal should be intelligibility, not a perfect imitation of a specific accent.

What really matters: consistency, not perfection

It’s more important to be consistent with an “r” sound (even if it’s not 100% native) than to vary randomly between different attempts—consistency helps your listeners get used to the way you speak.
